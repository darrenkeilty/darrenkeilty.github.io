Understanding Timeliness

A Visual Analysis of Daily Commutes

Project Overview

By Darren Keilty, Hassan Alvi, Caitlin McConnery
CPSC/DATA 482 â€“ Data Visualization Fundamentals (Fall 2025)

This project explores patterns of punctuality in everyday commutes using real-world data. With data collected over several weeks, we were able to examine how relative timliness was impacted by factors like transport mode, activity type, time of day, and mood. The goal was to practice and polish learned visualization techniques, and produce a polished dashboard that enables users to understand and improve their commuting behavior.

Key Questions Explored

  • Which activities tolerate lateness more than others?
  • How does mood before a commute affect arrival accuracy?
  • Which transport modes are the most reliable?
  • Are we typically early or late at certain times or days?

Key Findings

  • Train and driving were the most reliable transport modes, while bus trips showed the greatest variability.
  • Day-of-week effects were minimal, but clustering of lateness occurred within specific weeks.
  • Exams and appointments had the strictest punctuality expectations, while social and personal activities allowed more lateness.
  • Positive mood correlated with tighter arrival-time distributions and fewer extreme delays.

Conclusion: Timeliness is influenced less by the day itself and more by transport choice, activity type, and personal context. Visualizing commute behavior at multiple levels reveals practical insights that simple averages would otherwise hide.
